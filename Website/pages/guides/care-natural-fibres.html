Care Guide

How to Care for Natural Fibres

Natural fibres are genuinely better than synthetics — warmer, softer, longer-lasting. But they need a bit more thought. Once you know the basics, it's straightforward.

Washing Without Damage

Most natural fibre garments say "dry clean only" because it protects the brand from returns, not because hand-washing would ruin them. Wool and cashmere can be washed by hand in cool water with a gentle, pH-neutral detergent. The key: no agitation, no hot water, no wringing. Gently squeeze the water through. Don't rub.

Linen is more robust and can usually be machine-washed on a gentle, cool cycle. Cotton can be machine-washed warm, though washing at 30°C rather than 60°C extends its life considerably — the fibres break down more slowly.

Never soak wool or cashmere for extended periods. Five minutes in clean water is enough. Longer than that and the fibre structure starts to loosen.

The most overlooked tip: wash things less often. Most garments don't need washing after every wear. Airing them out — hanging them outside or in a ventilated room overnight — removes light odour and surface dust without any water.

Drying The Right Way

Heat is the enemy of natural fibres. Wool and cashmere should never go in the tumble dryer — they'll shrink, felt, and the fibres will begin to break down. Lay them flat to dry on a clean towel or a mesh drying rack, reshaped to their original dimensions while still damp.

Linen can air-dry on a hanger but may need a cool press to smooth out creases. Cotton can go in the dryer on a low heat setting, though air-drying extends its life.

Never hang a wet sweater. The weight of the water will stretch it at the shoulders, and once stretched that way, it won't go back.

Storage Between Seasons

Before you store anything for several months, make sure it's clean. Even tiny amounts of food residue or body oils can attract moths. Clean garments are much less appealing to them.

Fold knitwear rather than hanging it. Hanging causes wool and cashmere to stretch out of shape over time, especially at the shoulders.

  • Cedar blocks or sachets deter moths and need replacing or refreshing every year or so.
  • Lavender sachets work similarly and smell better, but need refreshing more often.
  • For long-term storage, loosely fold garments in acid-free tissue paper and keep them in a breathable cotton bag — not a plastic one, which traps moisture.

If you find a moth hole, the garment isn't ruined. A good repair tailor can fix most holes for under €20, and the result is usually invisible. It's worth the cost.
