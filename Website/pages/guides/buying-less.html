Buying Less, Choosing Better

We're a fashion brand, so it might seem strange to write a guide about buying less. But we'd rather you buy one piece and wear it for five years than buy five pieces and donate three of them in eighteen months.

Questions Before You Buy

The most useful thing you can do before buying a new garment is answer three questions honestly.

Do I already own something that does this job? If yes, is there a clear reason this new piece is better — different weight, better construction, fills a genuine gap? "I just want something new" is a valid feeling, but it's worth naming it clearly rather than dressing it up as a practical need.

Will I wear this at least thirty times? Thirty is a reasonable threshold. A piece worn thirty times at €100 is €3.33 per wear — roughly the same as renting it. Below thirty wears, most pieces are poor value. Above thirty, they become very good value. Apply this to expensive and inexpensive pieces alike.

Do I know what I'll wear it with? If you can't picture at least three combinations from pieces you already own, that's worth pausing on. A piece that only works in specific conditions will get worn rarely.

The Care Multiplier

How you care for clothes has a bigger effect on their lifespan than almost anything else you can do.

  • A piece washed at 30°C rather than 60°C experiences significantly less fibre breakdown over time.
  • A sweater laid flat to dry rather than tumble-dried retains its shape far longer.
  • A jacket brushed regularly with a clothes brush will need dry cleaning far less often.
  • Airing garments out between wears removes light odour and surface dust without any washing at all.

The simplest habit: wash things less often. Most garments don't need washing after every wear. Body odour and light surface dirt disappear when you hang something in a ventilated room overnight. Reducing wash frequency is one of the easiest ways to extend the life of a garment.

When To Repair, When To Replace

A small hole in a knit, a loose button, a worn collar — none of these are reasons to replace a garment. A good alteration tailor can fix most problems for under €20, and the result is usually invisible. It's worth the cost and almost always worth the effort of finding someone who does it well.

The threshold for replacement is when the structure of the garment has genuinely gone: the fabric is pilling heavily throughout, the shape has distorted and can't be recovered, or the fibre has worn thin. These are real end-of-life signs. A moth hole, a missing button, or a slightly worn cuff are not.

When a piece really is at the end of its life, look for a textile recycling drop-off rather than general waste. Most natural fibres can be broken down and reused. That's a better end than a landfill.
