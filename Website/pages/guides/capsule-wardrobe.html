Wardrobe Basics

The Capsule Wardrobe

The capsule wardrobe idea gets misrepresented. It's not about owning the minimum possible — it's about owning a set of pieces that covers almost every situation without gaps.

The Ten-Piece Idea

Ten is roughly the number of pieces it takes to cover most situations from Monday to Sunday, dressed up and dressed down. Not ten full outfits — ten individual pieces that combine into many more.

Here's one version of those ten pieces:

  • A white shirt (cotton poplin or oxford cloth)
  • A blue or navy shirt
  • A fine-gauge knit in a neutral colour
  • A heavier mid-layer — blazer or structured jacket
  • A coat
  • Two pairs of trousers: one smarter, one more relaxed
  • One pair of dark jeans
  • A pair of versatile shoes that works with most of the above
  • One accessory that ties your colour palette together — a belt, a scarf, or a bag

Everything else is supplementary. Add it gradually and only when you can see exactly where it fits.

What To Look For

When choosing each piece, there are three things worth prioritising above anything else.

Neutral colours. Each piece should combine with at least three others in the set. If something only works with one or two other things, it limits the number of combinations available and reduces how often it gets worn.

Natural fibres. Cotton, wool, linen, and cashmere look better, last longer, and age more gracefully than synthetics. They're usually more expensive at purchase, but cheaper per wear over five or ten years. The maths is straightforward.

Solid construction. Check whether seams are finished, buttons are attached firmly, and hems are even. These are signs that a manufacturer put care into the piece, which usually means the rest of the construction is also good. A piece that comes apart at a seam after three months is not a bargain at any price.

What To Skip

Two types of pieces tend to underperform in a capsule wardrobe.

Very trendy pieces. If the appeal is that it's the thing right now, it'll stop feeling right in a season or two. Capsule wardrobes need pieces that don't date. Classics in good fabrics age well. Current trends, almost by definition, do not.

Highly specific pieces. A shirt in a very particular print, trousers in a shade that doesn't go with anything else, a jacket that only works for one type of occasion. Specificity is fine in a larger wardrobe. In ten pieces, it creates gaps that force you to buy more.

Build slowly. A capsule wardrobe put together over two or three seasons is more coherent than one bought all at once, because you learn what you actually reach for and what stays on the hanger.
