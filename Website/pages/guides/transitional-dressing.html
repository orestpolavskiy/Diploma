Transitional Dressing

Dressing between seasons is one of the trickier things to get right. The temperature drops in the morning and climbs by afternoon. Here's how we think about it.

The Layering Principle

The trick with layering isn't adding more pieces — it's choosing pieces that work at two or three different temperatures. A linen shirt worn alone in summer can become the base layer under a fine-knit pullover in September. A blazer worn over a T-shirt in autumn becomes the middle layer under a heavier coat once it gets cold.

Think in terms of weight: light base, medium mid-layer, heavier outer. Each one should be slim enough to layer without bulking up at the shoulders or collar. If you can't move your arms comfortably with everything on, one of the layers is too heavy.

The other thing to keep in mind: the mid-layer does most of the thermal work. Don't underinvest in it.

Fabric Weights That Cross Seasons

Some fabrics are clearly seasonal: heavy wool melton, thick padding, open-weave linen for summer. But several sit in the middle, and that's where you want most of your wardrobe budget.

  • Cotton canvas works from spring through autumn. It doesn't overheat and holds its shape in wind.
  • Fine merino wool is warm worn alone but thin enough to layer without discomfort.
  • Mid-weight cashmere (around 2-ply) is one of the best transitional fabrics there is.
  • Poplin cotton in pale colours sits comfortably in both warmth and cold.

What doesn't cross seasons well: lightweight chiffon in autumn, heavy tweed in spring, or anything synthetic that traps heat and doesn't breathe.

Three Pieces That Do The Most Work

If you're building a transitional wardrobe from scratch, start here. Everything else can come later.

A mid-layer knit. Not too thick, not too thin. Fine-gauge merino or cashmere in a neutral — charcoal, navy, camel, or ivory. This will be your most-worn piece in any transitional month.

A structured outer layer. A tailored coat or a quality field jacket covers September to December. It shouldn't be so warm that it's uncomfortable at 15°C, but substantial enough to be useful at 5°C over layers.

A versatile trouser. Wool-blend in a relaxed cut takes you from the office to an evening out and pairs with everything. Avoid trousers that are too light or too formal — they limit the outfit rather than extend it.

Start with these three. Add pieces gradually and only when you can picture exactly where they fit into the rotation.
